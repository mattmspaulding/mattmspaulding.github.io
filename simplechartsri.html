SimpleChartsRI

Help Teachers Create Engaging Visualizations

Overview

SimpleChartsRI is a website I was given the task of creating while working on a grant under the National Science Foundation. I was hired as an intern for 10 weeks in the Summer of 2020. I was rehired at the end of the Summer, then hired again for a third time on a contract basis after my graduation in December. I had a lot of freedom working on this project and created all aspects of the site up until April 2021, when my contract ended and a new line of work began under my proposol.

When beginning my work on the site, my only limitations were that the site needed to address non tech-savvy high-school teachers and help them make charts. I hit the ground running and produced mockups and designs. Within three weeks, I had a functioning version of the website that I demoed to a group of high-school teachers during a workshop.

During that workshop, while working with teachers to create visualizations for classroom use and noticing the struggle to find datasets that would be simple and engaging for high-school students, I found the core identity of what would soon become SimpleChartsRI.

The Problem

Current tools didn't offer much when helping specific users. They were aimed towards a general audience. Teachers, who were already under constant time crunch, couldn't gain much of a benefit from chart making tools. These tools still required users to find the data themselves, which is the most timely part of making an engaging chart.

Approach

The site's ultimate focus is to help users create engaging charts quickly. This means users must be able to find engaging datasets through the website, and use those datasets to create a chart through the website. Because the project is aimed towards teachers who perhaps aren't tech-savvy, additional help should be given to notify them of potential mistakes that may make a chart ineffective.

My focus during my time on the project was to create a foundation for the site. So that's what I did. I created a way for users to upload their data and have that data get parsed into a format the charts (which I created using Chart.js) could understand. After completing that, it was mostly about customization options, handling errors, and creating a consistent format throughout every step of the user's chart-making process. The customization options I made were as follows:

1) Change Chart Type - dynamically create an overlapping chart by specifying each dataset's chart type, or change the whole thing at once.

2) Change Color Scheme - look at a preview of each color scheme and customize it to your liking.

3) Customize Labels - chart's title, x-axis label, and y-axis label.

4) Change Data - dynamically open up a spreadsheet that shows you all of the chart's data and lets you edit it.

What I Learned

This project gave me a passion for UX/UI design. It gave me an opportunity to work on a project and think about all of its parts — both the whole picture and every last miniscule detail. While it was a lot of work acting as the UX/UI Designer, Frontend Engineer, and Backend Engineer, it taught me an incredible amount. I found that I deeply enjoy thinking about how the user will interact with the interface and simplifying that experience into something pleasant.

Teamwork is Everything — It's easy to get absorbed in your own world when you're the only one working on a project. Having other perspectives can help you rethink things you may have overlooked. Having a well-rounded team with different backgrounds can only result in a more well-rounded product.

Testing is Also... Everything — Things go wrong. It's a natural part of the process. Thinking about potential problems before they happen can save future headaches. There can be backend problems with how data is gathered, problems with how that data is sent to the frontend, problems with the frontend... The list goes on and on. I learned how reguarly problems, even the same problems, can appear and reappear with every change to the codebase.

Passion is Crucial— Without it, I don't think I wouldn't have accomplished half of what I did on this project. Learning new technologies and focusing on a problem that I felt was crucial helped constantly motivate me. I went from knowing very little HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, to feeling comfortable implementing any and every feature.
