Help Teachers Create Engaging Visualizations

Overview

While working as a Research Technician on a National Science Foundation grant at Rhode Island College, I was given the task of creating a data visualization web-tool. I created an identity for the tool through market research, user surveys, and workshop observations. After that I built a list of requirements, created some mockups, and got to programming!

Overall, I had a lot of freedom working on this project and worked on the site up until around May 2021, after my contract ended and a new line of work began under my proposal. I learned a tremendous amount and through this project I developed a passion for focusing on the user's experience.

In October 2021, our research article discussing our tool was published by Frontiers Journal!

My Process

When I was initially introduced to the project I was told to create a data visualization tool. At first this concept was just an idea, but after asking clarifying questions I was able to identify requirements and outcome expectations.

After understanding stakeholder expectations, I began research to gauge interest in our tool. After analyzing surveys from around 20 high-school teachers, I found what features teachers wanted out of a data visualization tool.

Next, I reviewed literature and analyzed competing tools to see what was working and what needed improvement.

Within 3 weeks I created a working prototype of our tool and demoed it to teachers during a data visualization workshop. I was able to sit in with a group of teachers as they went about creating a lesson plan around data visualization. I assumed they would have the most difficulties with the visualization itself, but was wrong. Which was exciting! The teachers struggled instead with finding datasets that would be simple and engaging for their students. They asked questions like, "Would this be interesting to our students," "Is this data too complex," "Will our students have trouble cleaning this data," and "Will our students understand the ideas behind this data?" As soon as I heard these questions, I realized our site had to help solve this problem.

Through all of this research, I found an identity for our tool. It would save teachers time by providing many relevant datasets that are engaging because of their proximity (how close the data in the datasets are via location or time) to students.

When designing the tool, I followed a usability paper that gave optimal specifications, such as the best font size or button size, for elderly users. This design decision was meant to help all teachers, no matter their background in tech.

Throughout the project, I received feedback and made adjustments as needed. I adapted and took criticism well, and my goal and core focus was to create a tool that best served our users.

Key Takeaways

Insights from our Frontiers in Computer Science Article:

  • Teachers gave the tool an average usability score of 4.36/5.0.
  • They found it easy to use and navigate.
  • The teachers found that the tool could complement their lessons.
  • The teachers enjoyed having sample data they could instantly pull up, rather than having to search something themselves.
  • The teachers found they could implement the tool into student assignments and increase their students' data analysis skills.

Overall, the findings that Sean (the student who continued my work on the tool) was able to make confirmed the findings I had made early on. I think there is still room for additional feedback and more ways to receive feedback in a different setting that may be less prone to bias, but overall the results are reliable.

Lessons Learned

This project gave me a passion for UX/UI work, research, and working together with users to solve problems. It helped me realize the empathy I have for the user's experience. I learned many lessons throughout this time, but five stuck out the most.

Compensation is Crucial— Survey responses towards the end were low. When we weren't able to leverage our workshop to get survey responses, the surveys fell flat. At the time of the project, we were working through a pandemic. As shown through research, teachers were already strained on time. During a pandemic, this was even clearer. Asking teachers to essentially do volunteer work was a lot to ask. If I could do this again, I would have opted to leverage a portion of our budget towards compensating any of our target users who wanted to give us some feedback. After all, even beloved companies like Google pay their users for responses. This would have given us the feedback we needed to fix any problems in the site earlier.

Design Early and Receive Feedback Often— When I began the project, I had 3 weeks to work on the web-tool until I was expected to demo it during a workshop to our target users. For those 3 weeks I passionately worked on the project and build out a functional version of the site, which I thought would be more exciting for teachers to engage with. While I admire my drive, I think I should have spent more time on mockups and receiving feedback earlier rather than later. I didn't rely on design (utilizing programs like Figma) heavily enough, and often found myself tweaking CSS code and refreshing the page a dozen times to get something right. If I had spent time designing each page in Figma first, I could have received feedback on those designs (which are easier to tweak) and saved myself time.

Teamwork is Everything — It's easy to get absorbed in your own world when you're the only one working on a project. Having other perspectives can help you rethink things you may have overlooked. Having a well-rounded team with different backgrounds can only result in a more well-rounded product.

Testing is Also... Everything — Things go wrong. It's a natural part of the process. I learned how regularly problems, even the same problems, can appear and reappear with every change. I walked away from this project having a better appreciation but for the design phase.

Passion, Passion, Passion— Without it, I don't think I would have accomplished half of what I did on this project. Learning new technologies and focusing on a problem that I felt was important helped constantly motivate me. I went from knowing very little HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, to feeling comfortable implementing any feature.

Next Steps

I wrote about the future of the project in my Frontiers in Computer Science Article. To recap here, I envisioned the site having a larger selection of engaging data that teachers could directly use, as well more ways to streamline the data cleaning process. In addition to that, I wanted the site to have more resources for teachers to create effective charts and guide them along the way towards best practices. I wrote a proposal to extend the work on this tool, and that proposal was accepted. After another student's work, the datasets available on the site have increased massively.
