SimpleChartsRI

Help Teachers Create Engaging Visualizations

Challenge

Student engagement is crucial to memory retention. Visualizations can help engage students. However, it is timely. Finding, formatting, and displaying data can become a headache for a teacher who is already short on time. Lastly, current visualization tools can be confusing for non-tech-savvy teachers.

Solution

SimpleChartsRI is a free and easy-to-use web-tool that provides engaging datasets and relevant information for creating effective charts. Teachers rated the tool favorably (4.36/5.0) in terms of usability and identified it as a tool to complement their lessons.

My Impact

While working as a Research Technician on a National Science Foundation grant at Rhode Island College, I was given the task of creating a data visualization web-tool. I created an identity for the tool through market research, user surveys, and workshop observations. After that I built a list of requirements, created some mockups, and developed version 1.0.

My Process

When given the task of creating a data visualization tool, we didn't know what that would look like. After asking clarifying questions, analyzing surveys and competitors, I identified our tool's requirements and established how our tool could help.

Within 3 weeks I created a working prototype of our tool and demoed it to teachers during a data visualization workshop. I assumed the teachers would have the most difficulties with creating a visualization, but I was wrong. Which was exciting!

The teachers struggled with finding simple and engaging datasets. They asked the following questions:

  • "Would this be interesting to our students?"
  • "Is this data too complex?"
  • "Will our students have trouble cleaning this data?"
  • "Will our students understand the ideas behind this data?"

As soon as I heard these questions, I realized our site had to help solve this problem.

I researched what made effective visualizations and created an identity for our site. Our site would save teachers time by providing many relevant datasets that are engaging because of their proximity (how close the data in the datasets are via location or time) to students.

When designing the tool, I also followed a usability paper that gave optimal specifications, such as the best font size or button size, for elderly users, which complemented an initial design decision to create the web-tool for non-tech-savvy teachers.

Throughout the project, I received feedback and made adjustments as needed. I adapted and took criticism well. Overall, my goal was to create a tool that best served our users.

What I Learned

  • How to dig deeper into a problem, gain clarification, and employ the correct solution.
  • Effective communication comes from precise and well thought out points.
  • Empathizing with the user will always help you identify gaps in whatever you are working on.
  • Pressure helps me focus. Without knowing much about web development, I built a functional website within 3 weeks (under a deadline) while also handling many other tasks.
  • Teams are only healthy with constant feedback. Having others give feedback keeps your perspective fresh.
  • I enjoy communicating my work. I presented as a speaker, but also created posters, videos, and soundbites. I found myself editing videos down to every frame and using audio plugins to ensure my voice sounded as pleasant as possible, devoid of any crackles or background noise, so the audience could clearly understand my research.

Key Takeaways

Insights from our Frontiers in Computer Science Article:

  • Teachers gave the tool an average usability score of 4.36/5.0.
  • They found it easy to use and navigate.
  • The teachers found that the tool could complement their lessons.
  • The teachers enjoyed having sample data they could instantly pull up, rather than having to search something themselves.
  • The teachers found they could implement the tool into student assignments and increase their students' data analysis skills.

Next Steps

I wrote about the future of the project in our Frontiers in Computer Science Article. To recap, I envisioned the site having a larger selection of engaging data as well more ways to streamline the data cleaning process. In addition, I wanted the web-tool to guide teachers towards best practices. I wrote a proposal to extend the work on this tool, and that proposal was accepted. After another student's work, the datasets available on the site have increased massively.
