SimpleChartsRI

Help Teachers Create Engaging Visualizations

Overview

SimpleChartsRI is a data visualization web-tool I was given the task of creating while working on a grant under the National Science Foundation at Rhode Island College, initially as a Research Technician.

I shaped the site's direction through the following methods:

  • Surveys
  • Observations
  • Literature Review

I presented the web-tool (our research project) at the following events:

  • CCSCNE 2021 Conference
  • 2021 RI NSF EPSCoR Symposium
  • Data Visualization: The Great Synthesizer workshop
  • Inner team during weekly meetings

I also attended the Developing Soundbites Workshop W/ Metcalf Institute workshop.

Overall, I had a lot of freedom working on this project and worked on the site up until around May 2021, after my contract ended and a new line of work began under my proposal. I learned a tremendous amount and through this project I developed a passion for focusing on the user's experience.

The Problem

Current data visualization tools didn't offer much when helping specific users, especially teachers. They were aimed towards a general audience. Teachers, who were already under a constant time crunch, couldn't gain much from these tools. They still had to find good data themselves, which is the most timely part of making an engaging chart. These tools also didn't guide users towards best practices, which could result in ineffective visualizations.

During a data visualization workshop, I helped a group of teachers create a visualization that they would incorporate with a lesson plan. While working with them, I noticed how the teachers struggled to find datasets that would be simple and engaging for their high-school students. They asked questions like, "Would this be interesting to our students," "Is this data too complex," "Will our students have trouble cleaning this data," and "Will our students understand the ideas behind this data?" As soon as I heard those questions, I realized our site had to help fix this problem.

Research

The initial foundation of the site was influenced through surveys gathered from around 20 high-school teachers. This helped inform us on what options they were expecting in a data visualization tool and what would help them most. The aforementioned observations additionally helped with this foundation.

To influence the design and help form the idea further, I extensively researched and reviewed literature. I searched any and all information that helped charts engage their audience. I found a number of factors that played into how effective a visualization was. The main takeaway I found was centered around the idea of proximity.

This helped me guide the direction of SimpleChartsRI towards maintaining a collection of datasets that would be relevant to a Rhode Island high-school audience. This is what set apart our website from others. It aimed to have ready-to-go datasets that teachers could instantly use, without any need for searching or cleaning it themselves. Because of the idea of proximity, the datasets would have a higher chance at engaging students.

What I Learned

This project gave me a passion for UX/UI design. It gave me an opportunity to work on a project and think about all of its parts — both the whole picture and every last miniscule detail. It helped me realize the empathy I have for the user's experience. While it was a lot of work acting as the UX/UI Designer, Frontend Engineer, and Backend Engineer, it taught me an incredible amount, and I thrived off the challenge. I found that I deeply enjoy thinking about how the user will interact with the interface and simplifying that experience into something pleasant. I learned many lessons, but three stuck out the most.

Teamwork is Everything — It's easy to get absorbed in your own world when you're the only one working on a project. Having other perspectives can help you rethink things you may have overlooked. Having a well-rounded team with different backgrounds can only result in a more well-rounded product.

Testing is Also... Everything — Things go wrong. It's a natural part of the process. Thinking about potential problems before they happen can save future headaches. There can be backend problems with how data is gathered, problems with how that data is sent to the frontend, problems with the frontend... The list goes on and on. I learned how regularly problems, even the same problems, can appear and reappear with every change to the codebase. I walked away having a better appreciation not only for testing, but for the design phase in general. Spending more time on that phase can be crucial and save both time and money.

Passion is Crucial— Without it, I don't think I would have accomplished half of what I did on this project. Learning new technologies and focusing on a problem that I felt was crucial helped constantly motivate me. I went from knowing very little HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, to feeling comfortable implementing any and every feature.
